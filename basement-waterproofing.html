Why It Matters

A Wet Basement Is Never Just a Wet Basement

Water is patient. It works through hairline cracks, past aging membranes, and around failed drainage systems — quietly, consistently, until the damage becomes undeniable. By the time most homeowners call a basement waterproofing company, the problem has been building for months or years.

Basement moisture problems don't stop at stained walls or musty odours. Left unaddressed, water intrusion undermines your foundation's structural integrity, creates ideal conditions for mould growth, and steadily erodes the value of your home. A dry basement, on the other hand, is usable space, healthy air, and a foundation that stays solid for decades.

At AW Waterproofing Solutions, we've spent years working on foundations across Napanee, Belleville, Kingston, and the surrounding communities. We know what causes water seepage in basements in this region — the clay-heavy soils, the freeze-thaw cycling, the spring snowmelt pressure — and we know how to stop it permanently.

Warning Signs

Signs You May Need Basement Waterproofing

Some problems announce themselves loudly — a flooded floor after a heavy rain. Others are quieter, building slowly in corners and along floor joints where you might not look. These are the eight signs we see most often in homes that have basement moisture problems.

💧

Water Seepage Through Walls

Water pushing through porous concrete or block walls, often appearing as damp patches, streaks, or small trickles after rainfall. A clear sign of hydrostatic pressure at work.

🌫️

Damp or Sweating Walls

Walls that feel wet to the touch or show condensation even without visible water. Often caused by warm air meeting a cold foundation wall — but sometimes a sign of moisture migrating through the concrete itself.

🔓

Foundation Cracks

Vertical, horizontal, or diagonal cracks in poured concrete or block foundations. Horizontal cracks in particular can indicate lateral soil pressure and should never be ignored.

👃

Musty Odours

That persistent damp smell is caused by mould and mildew feeding on moisture. You may not see the mould yet — but if the smell is there, the conditions for growth are already present.

🦠

Mould or Mildew Growth

Mould colonies on walls, floor joists, or stored belongings are a serious health concern and a reliable indicator of chronic moisture. Cleaning the mould without fixing the moisture source is a temporary fix at best.

⬜

Efflorescence

White or grey chalky deposits on concrete or block walls. Efflorescence is caused by water dissolving salts in the concrete and depositing them on the surface as it evaporates — a reliable sign of water movement through your foundation.

🌊

Standing Water

Puddles on the basement floor after rain or snowmelt indicate that water is entering faster than it can drain — often through a floor crack, a compromised floor-wall joint, or a failed weeping tile system.

🌡️

High Humidity

A consistently humid basement — above 60% relative humidity — creates the perfect environment for mould, wood rot, and rust. If your dehumidifier is running constantly, that's a symptom, not a solution.

Root Causes

Common Causes of Basement Water Problems

Understanding why your basement leaks is the first step to fixing it properly. Treating the symptom without addressing the source is how homeowners end up paying for the same repair twice. Here are the causes we diagnose most frequently.

01

Hydrostatic Pressure

When soil surrounding your foundation becomes saturated — after heavy rain or snowmelt — water pressure builds against the outside of the wall. If the waterproof barrier has aged or failed, that pressure will find a way in.

02

Poor Grading & Drainage

If the ground around your home slopes toward the foundation rather than away from it, rainwater has nowhere to go but down — straight toward your basement wall. This is one of the most common and most overlooked contributors to basement leaks.

03

Foundation Cracks

Concrete cures and shrinks. Soil shifts and settles. Over years, even well-built foundations develop cracks — and any crack is a potential entry point for water. Foundation crack repair is often the most direct solution to a pinpointed leak.

04

Aging Waterproof Membranes

Most homes built before the 1990s were waterproofed with tar-based coatings that crack, become brittle, and fail over time. If your home is older and has never had exterior waterproofing updated, it is likely working with a compromised barrier — or none at all.

05

Heavy Rainfall Events

Intense rainfall overwhelms even good drainage systems. A single heavy storm can saturate soil faster than it can drain and push water through vulnerabilities in the foundation that were previously manageable.

06

Spring Snowmelt

In Ontario, the spring thaw is one of the highest-risk periods for basement flooding. Frozen ground prevents snowmelt from absorbing into the earth, directing it toward the lowest point — your foundation. Combined with a high water table, spring can be brutal on unprotected basements.

07

Clogged or Failed Weeping Tile

Weeping tile — perforated pipe installed around the base of your foundation — is designed to collect and redirect groundwater. Over decades, these systems can collapse, become root-infiltrated, or fill with silt. When the weeping tile fails, water has nowhere to go but up through your floor.

08

Window Wells & Hatch Drains

Basement window wells that aren't properly drained act as collection points for rainwater and leaves. A clogged or undersized window well drain can direct significant water volume directly against your foundation wall at a vulnerable point.

Our Services

Our Basement Waterproofing Solutions

No two basements are the same. The right waterproofing solution depends on your foundation type, the source of water entry, your soil conditions, and your budget. We assess every situation individually and recommend only what the problem actually requires — nothing more.

Exterior Basement Waterproofing

Exterior waterproofing is the most comprehensive approach to foundation waterproofing — and the most durable. We excavate around the perimeter of the foundation to expose the full height of the wall, then clean and prepare the surface for membrane application.

A Duraflex rubberized membrane is applied directly to the concrete or block wall, creating a seamless, fully bonded waterproof barrier. Over the membrane, we install a drainage board that directs water downward, away from the wall, and toward a refreshed weeping tile system at the base of the footing.

The backfill is then carefully compacted in layers to minimize future settlement. Done properly, an exterior waterproofing system stops water before it ever contacts your foundation — the most effective protection available.

Duraflex Membrane Drainage Board Weeping Tile

Interior Waterproofing

Interior waterproofing doesn't stop water from entering the foundation wall — but it intercepts it before it can cause damage and directs it safely away from your living space. It is less invasive than exterior waterproofing, can be completed without disrupting landscaping, and is often the most practical solution for older homes or situations where full excavation isn't warranted.

We install a drainage channel system along the perimeter of the basement floor, beneath the slab, that collects water seeping through the wall-floor joint and floor cracks and channels it to a sump pit. A high-quality sump pump then removes the collected water from the home.

Interior waterproofing is highly effective and, in many cases, is the recommended solution even when the budget for exterior work exists — particularly when water entry is through the floor or floor-wall joint rather than through the wall face itself.

Drainage Channel Sump Pump Minimal Disruption

Foundation Crack Repair

Not every basement water problem requires a full waterproofing system. When water is entering through a discrete crack in a poured concrete foundation, targeted crack repair is often the most efficient and cost-effective solution.

We use polyurethane or epoxy injection methods to fill and seal foundation cracks from the inside, following the crack through its full depth to create a permanent, flexible seal that moves with the foundation rather than cracking again. For cracks showing structural movement, we assess whether additional reinforcement is required before sealing.

Polyurethane Injection Epoxy Injection

Sump Pump Systems

A sump pump is the last line of defence in any interior waterproofing system. We install primary sump pumps sized to handle your home's water volume, paired with battery backup systems that keep your basement protected during power outages — which often coincide with the heaviest rainfall events.

If you already have a sump pump that's running constantly, cycling erratically, or failing to keep up, that's often a sign of an undersized pump, a drainage system that needs attention, or a pump that's reached the end of its service life. We assess the full system, not just the pump in isolation.

Primary & Battery Backup Correctly Sized

Drain Tile Systems

Weeping tile — the perforated drainage pipe installed at the base of your foundation footing — is a critical component of any foundation's long-term water management strategy. When existing weeping tile is clogged, collapsed, or missing, groundwater has no managed exit point and pressure against the foundation builds.

We replace failed weeping tile systems with properly sized, stone-encased perforated pipe that collects groundwater and directs it to daylight or to the sump system. New drain tile, combined with an exterior membrane or interior drainage channel, creates a complete, redundant water management system.

Certified System

Duraflex Waterproofing Systems

Not all waterproofing membranes are equal. Tar-based coatings — still used by some contractors today — become brittle with age, crack through freeze-thaw cycles, and lose adhesion to the foundation wall. When they fail, they fail silently, and the water returns.

Duraflex is a rubberized, polymer-modified membrane that applies directly to concrete or masonry and remains permanently flexible. It doesn't crack. It doesn't peel. And unlike sheet membrane systems that rely on seams and adhesive, Duraflex creates a continuous, seamless barrier across the entire foundation surface.

We chose Duraflex because it outperforms alternatives in the conditions Ontario foundations actually face — deep frost, high hydrostatic pressure, and decades of use. We are certified installers, which means we have the training to apply it correctly and the accountability that comes with that certification.

Field Insights

From Our Experience

After working on hundreds of basements across this region, certain patterns emerge. These aren't things you'll find in a brochure — they're the kind of observations that come from years of diagnosing, fixing, and following up on real basement water problems.

From our experience

Many homeowners wait too long before addressing small leaks. What starts as a damp patch in the corner — easy to ignore through the summer months — becomes a significant repair by the time a second or third winter has pushed it further. The hairline crack that lets in a trickle today is the same crack that opens under frost pressure next March. We've seen it consistently enough that we'd rather say it plainly: early intervention saves money.

From our experience

Musty odours are one of the first warning signs homeowners notice — and one of the most consistently dismissed. It's easy to attribute a basement smell to old storage or poor ventilation. But in the majority of cases where we've been called in because of a persistent odour, we find active moisture behind walls, under flooring, or along the floor-wall joint. The smell is the mould announcing itself before it becomes visible. Don't wait for visual confirmation.

From our experience

Spring thaw and heavy rainfall together are responsible for more basement flooding calls than any other cause in this region. When frozen ground can't absorb snowmelt and the water table is already elevated from winter accumulation, even well-maintained foundations come under pressure they weren't designed to handle alone. Homes that sail through dry summers discover their drainage vulnerabilities in April and May every single year without fail.

From our experience

Homeowners are frequently surprised to learn that their sump pump — running perfectly — is not the source of their dry basement, but a symptom of a larger drainage problem. A pump that runs every ten minutes isn't a sign of a great system. It's a sign that enormous volumes of groundwater are reaching the sump pit, which means the drainage system surrounding the foundation is either overwhelmed or absent. The pump should be a backup, not the primary defence.

From our experience

Horizontal cracks in block foundation walls are the ones that concern us most. Vertical cracks can often be injected and monitored. But a horizontal crack — particularly one that runs across the middle or lower third of a block wall — indicates lateral soil pressure flexing the wall inward. That's a structural issue, not just a waterproofing issue, and it needs to be assessed sooner rather than later. We've walked into homes where the bulge in the wall was visible from across the room. Those don't wait.

From our experience

The cheapest quote almost always costs more in the long run. We say this not to discourage comparison shopping — you absolutely should get multiple estimates — but because we've been called in to redo work from contractors who cut corners on membrane preparation, skipped drainage board, used undersized drain tile, or applied the coating to a wet wall. The re-do costs more than the original job would have, and the homeowner has now paid twice for the same problem.

From our experience

Efflorescence is often more informative than the water stain it accompanies. The white, chalky deposit tells us not just that water is moving through the wall, but which direction and at what rate. A fresh bloom of efflorescence after a rain event points to active seepage under hydrostatic pressure. Old, dry efflorescence with no recent activity might indicate a past problem that's been resolved by grading or drainage changes. Reading those patterns accurately changes the diagnosis and the solution.

From our experience

Many homes in this area have weeping tile systems that are thirty, forty, or fifty years old and have never been inspected. Original clay or concrete weeping tile from older construction can collapse, fill with roots, or simply disintegrate with age. When we scope old weeping tile, we frequently find sections that are completely blocked or structurally failed — and the homeowner has no idea their drainage system has been non-functional for years. A failed weeping tile system almost always shows up eventually in the basement.

From our experience

Dehumidifiers treat the symptom, not the cause. They remove moisture from basement air, which does help protect stored belongings and reduce mould risk in the short term. But a dehumidifier that needs to be emptied daily, or one that simply can't keep humidity below 60%, is telling you something important: there is more moisture entering the basement than should be entering it. The dehumidifier is working against an active water intrusion problem, and it will keep losing that fight until the source is addressed.

From our experience

Homeowners who have had their basements waterproofed consistently tell us the same thing afterward: they wish they'd done it sooner. The anxiety that comes with every heavy rain, the reluctance to finish the basement or store anything valuable down there, the recurring cost of managing moisture rather than fixing it — those things disappear after proper waterproofing. A dry basement changes how you use your home. It's not a small thing. It's a fundamental improvement to how the house works and feels year-round.

Making the Right Choice

The right solution depends on your specific situation. This comparison covers the practical differences between the two main approaches so you can have an informed conversation with us at your free estimate.

Factor Exterior Waterproofing Interior Waterproofing
How it works Stops water at the source by sealing the outside of the foundation wall with a waterproof membrane Intercepts water that enters the foundation and manages it via drainage channels and a sump pump
Disruption to landscaping High — requires full perimeter excavation Minimal — all work is done inside the basement
Disruption to interior Minimal — outside work only Moderate — perimeter of basement floor is broken and replaced
Addresses wall seepage Yes — prevents water contact with the wall Manages it — captures water at floor level before it spreads
Addresses floor seepage Indirectly, via improved drainage at footing Yes — drainage channel system collects floor seepage directly
Best for foundation cracks Yes — membrane seals over cracks from outside Manages water from cracks rather than sealing them
Addresses structural crack movement Membrane is flexible but structural issues need separate assessment No — does not address structural concerns

Exterior waterproofing stops water at the source by excavating around the foundation and applying a waterproof membrane to the outside of the wall. It prevents water from ever contacting the foundation surface. Interior waterproofing manages water that enters the basement by directing it to a drainage channel and sump pump before it can damage your floor or belongings. Both are effective — the right choice depends on your home's specific situation, which we assess during your free estimate.

A properly installed exterior waterproofing system using a Duraflex membrane can last 20 to 30 years or more. Interior drainage systems are designed as permanent installations — the channel and pipe don't wear out, and sump pumps are replaced as needed (typically every 7–10 years). Longevity depends heavily on quality of materials and skill of installation, which is why we don't cut corners on either.

In most cases, standard home insurance policies do not cover waterproofing work, as it is considered a maintenance issue rather than sudden accidental damage. However, damage caused by a sudden and accidental event (a pipe burst, for example) may be covered. Some policies include overland water or sewer backup coverage that can apply in specific flood situations. We recommend checking your policy and speaking with your insurance broker — every situation is different.

The simplest test: if the problem gets worse during or after rain, it's water intrusion. If it tracks with summer humidity, ventilation may help. When in doubt, have someone assess it — there's no cost for an honest opinion from us.
