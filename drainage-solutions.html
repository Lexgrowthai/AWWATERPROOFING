The Root of Most Water Problems

Drainage Is Where Basement Water Problems Begin

Most water problems in basements and foundations aren't really waterproofing failures — they're drainage failures. When water has nowhere to go, it goes into your basement. Proper drainage — weeping tile, grading, sump pump systems, interior channels — manages water at every point around the home before it can build up pressure against the foundation.

A foundation wall can only resist so much. When the soil surrounding it becomes saturated and has no drainage outlet, hydrostatic pressure mounts until water finds a path through — through cracks, through the floor-wall joint, through the wall face itself. No membrane, no matter how well applied, is designed to hold against sustained hydrostatic pressure without drainage relief behind it. Drainage and waterproofing are not alternatives to each other; they are two parts of the same system, and drainage comes first.

We design and install complete drainage systems for homes across Napanee, Belleville, Kingston, and surrounding communities. That means assessing the full drainage picture — grading, downspouts, soil type, water table conditions, existing weeping tile — and recommending a system that addresses what is actually causing the water problem, not just what is most visible.

Whether your home needs exterior weeping tile replaced, an interior drainage channel installed, a sump pump upgraded, or simply the grading corrected around the foundation, we can assess the situation and give you a straight answer about what it will take to keep water where it belongs: outside.

Warning Signs

Signs You Have a Drainage Problem

Drainage problems don't always present as visible flooding. Many of the clearest indicators are subtle patterns — timing, location, frequency — that tell an experienced eye exactly where the drainage system has broken down.

💧

Water After Every Rain

Basement gets wet consistently after rainfall, not just during heavy events. This is the classic sign of poor surface drainage or failed weeping tile — water is reliably reaching the foundation every time rain falls.

🌊

Pooling Water Near Foundation

Surface water that pools near the house rather than draining away creates hydrostatic pressure against the foundation wall and continuously increases soil moisture against the foundation surface.

🪣

Sump Pump Running Constantly

A sump pump that cycles frequently — especially during dry periods — means groundwater is reaching the pit faster than it should. The pump is compensating for a drainage problem, not solving it.

🚿

Wet Window Wells

A window well that fills with water during rain because drainage is blocked or absent will eventually allow water to seep into the basement through the window frame and surrounding masonry.

⬜

Efflorescence on Walls

White mineral deposits on basement walls confirm that water is moving through the wall. This is almost always a sign that soil outside is saturated with no drainage relief — a drainage problem, not just a waterproofing failure.

🌿

Soggy or Eroded Soil

Ground near the foundation that stays wet for days after rain, develops moss, or shows signs of erosion indicates water is collecting where it shouldn't — likely due to negative grading or blocked surface drainage.

🏚️

Settling or Heaving in Yard

Patches of yard that sink or heave near the foundation can indicate a failed or collapsed weeping tile allowing the surrounding soil to saturate, shift, and lose its structural stability over time.

🌫️

Chronic Basement Humidity

Even without visible water, a basement that never feels dry — that always has a damp, musty quality — indicates ongoing moisture migration from poorly drained surrounding soil pressing against the foundation.

Root Causes

What Causes Drainage Problems Around Foundations

The same external symptom — water in the basement — can stem from several different drainage failures. Identifying the actual cause determines the right solution. These are the drainage failures we encounter most consistently on homes across this region.

01

Failed Weeping Tile

The original clay tile or perforated pipe around the foundation has collapsed, silted up, or been blocked by root infiltration. The drainage system that was supposed to carry groundwater away from the foundation is no longer functioning, allowing water to build up against the wall with no outlet.

02

Negative Grading

Soil around the home slopes toward the foundation rather than away from it, directing surface runoff to pool against the wall. This is extremely common — soil settles over time, and what was once proper grading can become negative grading over decades without correction.

03

Blocked or Missing Downspout Extensions

Roof drainage empties directly at the foundation rather than being discharged away from the house. A single downspout can concentrate hundreds of gallons of water against the foundation during a typical rain event — a primary cause of localized basement water problems on one wall.

04

High Water Table

In some areas, the natural water table rises seasonally to near-foundation depth. When this occurs, the drainage system must actively manage groundwater continuously — not just during rain events. A standard drainage system may be undersized or improperly designed for these conditions.

05

Compacted or Clay-Heavy Soil

Soil that doesn't absorb or transmit water well — common in parts of Eastern Ontario — holds moisture in contact with the foundation for extended periods after every rain event. Clay soil can essentially act as a water reservoir around the foundation, maintaining pressure long after rainfall has ended.

06

No Interior Drainage System

Without an interior drainage channel at the floor-wall joint, any water that does enter the foundation through the wall or floor has nowhere managed to go. It spreads across the floor, wicks into any finished materials, and causes damage that accumulates with every wet event.

07

Undersized or Malfunctioning Sump Pump

A pump that can't keep up with peak water volume, that lacks a battery backup for power outages, or that has failed mechanically becomes a single point of failure for the entire drainage system. A drainage system is only as reliable as its ability to discharge the water it collects.

08

Disconnected or Poorly Routed Drainage

A drainage system is only as good as where it discharges. Systems that drain to a blocked outlet, an overwhelmed catch basin, or back into the soil near the foundation fail to move water away from the home regardless of how well the collection portion of the system is installed.

Our Services

Our Drainage Solutions

We don't install drainage components in isolation. We design systems that work together — exterior tile, interior channel, sump pump, surface grading — so there are no gaps in the water management picture around your home.

Weeping Tile Replacement (Exterior)

The original foundation drainage system in most Ontario homes. We excavate around the perimeter, remove the failed tile, install new perforated pipe wrapped in filter fabric to prevent silting, bed it in clear stone, and connect it to a proper outlet or sump basin. This is the most thorough drainage solution available because it intercepts water before it reaches the foundation wall.

When we perform exterior weeping tile work, we also assess the foundation surface and recommend membrane application where needed — the excavation provides the access for both drainage and waterproofing work simultaneously, which is more efficient and cost-effective than doing them separately.

Perforated Pipe Filter Fabric Clear Stone Bed Full Perimeter

Interior Drainage Channel System

Installed inside the basement at the floor-wall joint, this system collects water that enters the foundation and channels it to a sump pump. It's less disruptive than exterior excavation — no digging outside the home — and highly effective for managing water that enters through the wall or floor. We break a narrow channel in the floor perimeter, install drainage pipe in a clear stone bed, and restore the concrete over it. Water that enters the wall or floor is captured and removed before it can spread across the floor or wick into finished materials.

Interior drainage is the right choice when exterior excavation isn't practical due to decks, walkways, landscaping, or site constraints — and it is also the most direct solution when water enters primarily through the floor or the floor-wall joint rather than through the wall face.

Sump Pump Systems

A drainage system without a reliable pump is incomplete — it collects water but has no way to remove it. We install primary sump pumps sized to the actual water volume conditions of your site, not a generic residential specification. We also strongly recommend battery backup systems for every installation, because power outages and storm events occur simultaneously, and a primary pump without backup fails exactly when it is needed most.

If your existing pump is cycling constantly or struggling to keep up, we diagnose the drainage system as a whole before recommending a replacement. A pump that runs non-stop is almost always a sign that the drainage system feeding it has a problem — more water arriving than it should manage — and replacing the pump alone doesn't address that root cause.

Surface Grading & Downspout Correction

Often the simplest and most cost-effective fix for drainage problems — and frequently overlooked. Correcting the slope of soil around the foundation so water flows away rather than toward the wall, extending downspouts to discharge at least six feet from the foundation, and adding window well drains can significantly reduce water pressure on the foundation without any excavation.

We evaluate surface drainage conditions during every site assessment. In many cases, grading corrections and downspout extensions are recommended alongside other drainage work — they reduce the volume of water arriving at the foundation drainage system, making every other component of the system more effective.

Regrading Downspout Extensions Surface Drainage

Window Well Drainage

Window wells that collect standing water rather than draining it are a common and often overlooked source of basement window leaks. Water that sits in a window well finds its way through the window frame, the surrounding masonry, and the wall assembly — often causing wood rot and framing damage that isn't visible until it's extensive.

We install properly sized window wells with gravel drainage beds connected to the foundation drainage system, preventing water from pooling against the window frame. Where access allows, we connect window well drainage directly to the weeping tile system so that any water entering the well is carried away at the foundation level.

The System We Use

Duraflex Drainage Board: Where Drainage Meets Waterproofing

When we perform exterior foundation work, drainage and waterproofing are installed together — and the connection between them is the Duraflex drainage board. Applied over the waterproof membrane, the drainage board creates an air and drainage gap between the soil and the membrane surface. Rather than sitting in direct contact with saturated soil under full hydrostatic pressure, the membrane is protected behind this gap while water is directed downward to the weeping tile at the footing.

Without drainage board, even a high-quality membrane faces the full weight and pressure of saturated backfill soil. With drainage board in place, that pressure is relieved — water runs freely down the drainage gap to the tile, and the membrane faces soil rather than sustained water pressure. This is why drainage board is not an optional add-on in a properly designed exterior waterproofing system: it is the mechanism that makes the weeping tile and membrane work together as a system.

We are certified Duraflex installers. That means we understand how to integrate the membrane, the drainage board, and the weeping tile as a coordinated system — not three separate products installed without regard for how they interact.

Common Questions

Drainage Questions We Hear Every Day

These are the questions homeowners ask us most when they call about water around their foundation. If yours isn't here, call us — we give straight answers.

Weeping tile is the perimeter drainage system installed at the base of your foundation footing. Originally made from clay sections, later from concrete pipe, and today from perforated plastic, its job is to collect groundwater and direct it away from the foundation before it builds pressure against the wall. Signs of failure include a sump pump that runs constantly, basement flooding during or after rain, wet walls consistently after rainfall, and soil settlement or heaving near the foundation. Camera inspection through a cleanout or sump pit is the most reliable way to confirm the condition of existing tile — we use this regularly during site assessments.

Exterior weeping tile intercepts groundwater at the base of the foundation before it can build pressure against the wall — it is a preventive system. An interior drainage system is installed inside the basement and collects water that has already entered the foundation, channelling it to a sump pump before it can spread across the floor. Exterior tile is the more comprehensive approach because it addresses the source of pressure; interior drainage manages water after it enters but keeps the basement dry. Both systems are effective; the right choice depends on access, budget, and the specific water intrusion pattern in your home.

The right sump pump size depends on the volume of water your drainage system collects during peak conditions — typically during heavy rain events or spring thaw. Most residential installations use pumps in the 1/3 to 1/2 horsepower range, capable of moving 30 to 60 gallons per minute. However, homes with high water tables, large drainage areas, or clay-heavy soil conditions may require higher-capacity pumps. We size pumps based on the actual drainage system design and the site's water conditions, not a one-size-fits-all residential specification. An undersized pump in a high-demand situation is a reliability problem waiting to happen.

Yes — we recommend battery backup systems for virtually every sump pump system. The reason is straightforward: power outages and severe storms happen at the same time. A primary pump that loses power during the heaviest rain event of the year provides no protection exactly when it is needed most. Battery backup systems run independently of the electrical grid and can operate for many hours during an outage. The cost of a backup system is a fraction of the cost of a flooded basement, and the peace of mind is worth considerably more. We include battery backup in our standard sump pump recommendations.

Exterior weeping tile replacement involves excavating around the foundation perimeter — the most time-consuming part of the process. For a typical home, full perimeter exterior weeping tile installation takes three to five days depending on the perimeter length, soil conditions, and access to all sides of the foundation. Interior drainage channel installation is faster — typically one to two days for most homes — because it eliminates the excavation requirement. We provide a clear project timeline in your written estimate before work begins and keep you updated as work proceeds.

Interior drainage systems do require breaking a narrow channel in the perimeter of the basement floor slab in order to install the drainage pipe below the floor level. There is no way to install a properly functioning interior drainage channel without this step. However, the disruption is contained — typically a strip of six to twelve inches along the perimeter walls — and the concrete is restored after the drainage pipe and stone bed are in place. The finished result is a clean, solid floor with the drainage system completely concealed below it. Most homeowners are surprised by how minimal the disruption is relative to what it achieves.

Downspout extensions should discharge water at least six feet from the foundation — and further is better when site conditions allow. The goal is to move roof drainage beyond the backfill zone around the foundation, where soil is more permeable and water can easily reach the foundation wall. Where six feet is not practical due to lot size or landscaping, underground downspout drains can carry water further through a buried pipe. We frequently identify insufficient downspout discharge as a primary or contributing cause of basement water problems during our site assessments — it is one of the simplest and most cost-effective things to correct.

The lifespan of a weeping tile system depends heavily on the pipe material and installation quality. Original clay tile systems from homes built before the 1970s are typically at or well past the end of their service life — 50 to 60 years is the practical limit, and root infiltration and joint separation often shorten that. Concrete pipe systems from more recent decades typically last 30 to 40 years. Modern perforated plastic pipe, properly wrapped in filter fabric to prevent silting, is designed to last 50 years or more when correctly installed. A camera inspection gives a definitive assessment of any existing system's condition and prevents unnecessary replacement of serviceable tile.

Drainage installation costs vary widely by system type and scope. Downspout extension and surface grading corrections are typically the least expensive, ranging from a few hundred to a few thousand dollars. Interior drainage channel systems for an average home typically range from $5,000 to $15,000. Exterior weeping tile replacement with excavation ranges from $10,000 to $25,000 or more depending on perimeter length and site access. We provide free written estimates with no obligation — so you know exactly what you're looking at before any decision is made.

Simple surface drainage corrections — extending downspouts, adding splash blocks, or regrading soil away from the foundation — are within the ability of most homeowners and can make a meaningful difference. More involved work — interior drainage channel installation, sump pump sizing and installation, or exterior weeping tile replacement — requires proper knowledge of drainage gradients, pipe sizing, stone bed installation, and concrete work. Improperly installed drainage systems can make water problems worse by collecting water with no proper outlet, or by introducing new paths for water to enter the foundation. For anything beyond basic surface drainage, a professional assessment is a worthwhile first step before beginning work.
