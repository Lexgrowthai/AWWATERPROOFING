The Most Practical Solution for Most Homeowners

Interior Waterproofing: Managing Water Before It Becomes Damage

Interior basement waterproofing is the most practical solution for a majority of homeowners dealing with water in their basement. Rather than stopping water before it enters — which requires exterior excavation — interior waterproofing intercepts water the moment it enters the foundation, collects it in a below-floor drainage system, and pumps it safely away.

The result is a permanently dry basement without tearing up landscaping, without weeks of exterior work, and at a cost significantly lower than full exterior excavation. Interior systems work alongside the natural behaviour of the foundation rather than fighting it. When water enters through a wall crack, through the floor-wall joint, or through the slab itself, an interior drainage system has a managed path ready and waiting — the water goes exactly where the system directs it, not across your floor.

Interior waterproofing is not a compromise or a workaround. It is a purpose-built engineering solution designed specifically for the conditions most Canadian basements face: seasonal water table rises, spring melt events, and the cumulative effect of decades of hydrostatic pressure on aging concrete. A properly designed and installed interior system outperforms exterior approaches in several specific water entry scenarios, particularly floor-wall joint seepage and floor slab seepage from below.

We install interior waterproofing systems throughout Napanee, Belleville, Kingston, Deseronto, Bath, and surrounding communities. Every system is designed around the specific water entry patterns in your basement — not a generic perimeter channel installed identically in every home. We assess how water is entering, where it's coming from, and what drainage capacity the system needs to handle your property's actual conditions before a single cut is made in the floor.

Is This the Right Solution for You?

Signs Interior Waterproofing Is the Right Solution

Interior drainage is the appropriate solution for most residential water intrusion situations — but understanding which specific conditions it addresses best helps homeowners make the right choice. These are the patterns that point directly to interior waterproofing as the correct approach.

💧

Water at the Floor-Wall Joint

The most common entry point in poured concrete basements. Water seeping at the base of the wall where it meets the floor is exactly what interior drainage is designed to intercept — a channel at that joint captures it before it spreads across the floor.

🌊

Water Through the Floor Slab

Hydrostatic pressure forcing water up through cracks in the concrete floor is controlled by a sub-slab drainage system that relieves the pressure beneath the slab before it can push water up into the basement.

🔓

Multiple Wall Cracks Seeping

When several cracks are seeping simultaneously, crack-by-crack injection becomes expensive and impractical. An interior system handles all of them with a single installation — water from any crack point flows into the drainage channel rather than onto the floor.

🏚️

Exterior Excavation Not Feasible

When a deck, driveway, landscaping, or tight lot access prevents exterior work, interior waterproofing is the solution that provides equivalent results without requiring excavation. No disruption to the yard, the driveway, or the landscaping you've invested in.

💰

Cost-Effective Solution Needed

Interior waterproofing provides permanent, reliable results at a fraction of the cost of full exterior excavation. When budget matters and excavation isn't required by the specific water entry pattern, interior drainage is the right answer — not the cheaper compromise.

⬜

Porous Block Foundation Walls

Concrete block walls absorb and transmit water across the entire wall face rather than through specific cracks. An interior system with wall panels manages this distributed seepage without attempting to seal every mortar joint and block face on the wall.

🌫️

Chronic Basement Humidity

A basement that never feels dry even without visible water is experiencing moisture migration through the wall and floor assembly. Interior drainage combined with a dehumidifier controls this effectively, creating a basement environment suitable for storage and living use.

🪣

Sump Pit With No Drainage System

If a sump pump is running frequently but there is no drainage channel feeding it, water is finding its own unmanaged paths to the pit. A proper interior system channels that water efficiently and reduces the stress and wear on the pump itself.

Root Causes

Why Water Enters Basements — and How Interior Drainage Addresses Each

Interior waterproofing is effective against a wide range of water entry causes because it manages water after entry rather than relying on a single exterior barrier. Understanding what is driving water into your specific basement helps us design the most effective system for your home.

01

Hydrostatic Pressure

Saturated soil pushes water through any available opening in the foundation wall or slab under continuous pressure. Interior drainage relieves this pressure by providing a managed path for water to follow — into the drainage channel and away from the basement — rather than through the floor or wall face.

02

Floor-Wall Joint Separation

The joint between the foundation wall and the concrete floor slab is a common weak point that opens over time with settlement and thermal movement. Water under pressure follows this gap into the basement. An interior drainage channel positioned at this joint intercepts the water at its primary entry point.

03

Wall Porosity

Concrete block and older poured concrete walls become increasingly porous with age, allowing water to migrate through the wall material itself rather than through specific cracks. Interior drainage with wall panels addresses this distributed seepage by creating a managed drainage path along the interior wall face.

04

Failed or No Exterior Waterproofing

Homes with deteriorated dampproofing or no exterior system at all have nothing stopping water from reaching the foundation from outside. Interior drainage provides the protection layer that was never present — or has ceased to function — on the exterior of the wall.

05

High Seasonal Water Table

In many parts of Lennox and Addington and Hastings counties, the water table rises seasonally to near-foundation level, creating pressure regardless of surface drainage. An interior system designed for the peak water volume conditions of a given property keeps the basement dry even when the water table is at its highest.

06

Floor Slab Cracks

Cracks in the basement floor slab allow groundwater under hydrostatic pressure to push up into the basement. Sub-slab drainage relieves the pressure beneath the slab and intercepts water before it reaches floor level, making sealing individual floor cracks unnecessary and more durable long-term.

07

Wall Tie Rod Holes

In poured concrete walls, the tie rods used during forming are removed after the concrete sets and the holes are patched. These patches often deteriorate with age and become active leak points. When multiple tie rod holes are seeping, the drainage channel captures water from each one as part of the overall system rather than requiring individual repairs to each hole.

08

Window Well Overflows

Improperly drained window wells collect water that then seeps through the window frame and down the wall into the basement. Interior drainage captures this wall seepage as part of the perimeter system, and we address window well drainage directly as part of the assessment to reduce the water volume entering at that point.

What We Install

Our Interior Waterproofing Solutions

Interior waterproofing is a system of components working together, not a single product. The right combination depends on how water is entering your basement, the volume of water involved, and your foundation type. We design the system around your specific conditions.

Interior Drainage Channel Installation

A narrow trench is cut in the concrete floor around the interior perimeter of the basement at the base of the wall. Perforated drainage pipe is installed in a bed of clear stone, and the floor is then restored with new concrete poured over top. Water that enters the wall or floor-wall joint flows into this channel and is directed to the sump basin — before it can spread across the floor or damage anything in the basement.

The drainage channel is the core component of any interior system. It creates a permanent, managed collection path for water from any wall seepage point along the perimeter. Once installed and covered with concrete, it is invisible and requires no maintenance beyond keeping the sump pump operational.

Perforated Pipe Clear Stone Floor Restored Permanent System

Sub-Slab Drainage for Floor Seepage

When water is coming up through the floor slab itself, a sub-slab drainage system relieves the hydrostatic pressure that causes it. Drainage channels are installed beneath the floor level and connected to the sump system, intercepting water before it can push upward through the slab.

Sub-slab drainage is particularly effective in homes where the groundwater table seasonally rises near or above the base of the foundation slab. By providing the water a managed path below the floor, the upward pressure that causes floor seepage is eliminated. Attempting to seal floor cracks against continuous upward hydrostatic pressure is rarely successful long-term — relieving the pressure itself is the correct approach.

Pressure Relief Below-Floor Drainage Sump Connected

Sump Pump System

The drainage channel delivers water to a sump basin from which a properly sized sump pump discharges it away from the home. We install sump pumps matched to the water volume conditions of each specific property — an undersized pump running continuously is a system design failure, not a pump failure.

We strongly recommend battery backup systems on every interior waterproofing installation and include them as standard in our designs. Power outages and major rain events happen at the same time — they are caused by the same weather system. A backup pump is the difference between a dry basement and a flooded one during an outage. This is not an optional add-on; it is a critical component of a properly designed interior waterproofing system.

Properly Sized Battery Backup Discharge Line

Wall Waterproofing Panels

In porous block walls or walls with distributed seepage across the face, dimple mat waterproofing panels can be installed on the interior wall surface. These create a drainage gap between the wall and the finished space, directing water that passes through the wall downward to the floor drainage channel rather than allowing it to spread into the room or behind finished wall material.

Wall panels are particularly valuable in concrete block foundations where water migrates through the block material itself and through mortar joints across a large area — not just at discrete cracks. They work in combination with the floor drainage channel to create a complete interior water management system for the wall face and the floor simultaneously.

Dimple Mat Block Foundation Solution Channel Integrated

Crack Repair in Combination

Active point leaks from specific cracks are addressed with targeted polyurethane injection before the drainage system is installed. Combining crack injection with interior drainage gives the most complete result: point leaks sealed at their source, with a drainage system in place as a backup for any future seepage from any wall location.

This combined approach is particularly effective when a basement has one or two prominent leaking cracks alongside general wall moisture or floor-wall joint seepage. The crack injection addresses the highest-volume entry points directly, while the drainage system manages any remaining seepage and provides long-term protection against new entry points that may develop with time.

Interior + Exterior

How Interior Waterproofing Complements Duraflex Exterior Systems

Some homes need both interior and exterior protection — and when they do, the two systems work together to provide the most complete foundation protection available. Interior drainage and exterior Duraflex membrane are not competing solutions; they address the problem from two sides of the same wall.

Where an exterior Duraflex membrane applied to the foundation wall prevents water from penetrating the wall face from outside, an interior drainage system manages any water that does enter — through the floor-wall joint, through the slab, or through any point that the exterior membrane does not cover. Together, they create a redundant protection strategy: the exterior membrane is the primary barrier, and the interior drainage system is the backup that ensures a dry basement even if any water gets through.

In practice, many homes benefit from this combined approach. A home with a block foundation where the exterior has been addressed with Duraflex membrane may still benefit from interior wall panels and a drainage channel to manage residual moisture migration through the block material itself. A home with a good exterior membrane may have floor seepage from sub-slab hydrostatic pressure that only interior sub-slab drainage can address. We assess each home individually and recommend the combination of systems — or single approach — that matches the actual conditions.
