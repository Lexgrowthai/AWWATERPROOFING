Why the Material You Choose Changes Everything

Why the Material You Choose Changes Everything

When a waterproofing system fails, the failure usually isn't the installer's technique — it's the material that gave out. Tar-based dampproofing coatings crack. Thin spray membranes don't bond properly to damp concrete. Rigid coatings can't flex with foundation movement and develop gaps over time. The material is the system, and the system is what stands between your foundation and the water table.

At AW Waterproofing Solutions, we specify Duraflex on every project that involves a foundation membrane. Not because it's convenient — it's not the cheapest option — but because it performs. It bonds to concrete that is still damp. It remains flexible through Ontario's freeze-thaw cycles. It fills surface voids and irregularities rather than bridging over them. And it's the same material standard used in new construction by builders who understand what foundations face over 30, 40, 50 years.

Duraflex is a rubberized polymer waterproofing system applied directly to the exterior or interior face of the foundation wall. Unlike dampproofing — which only resists moisture vapour — Duraflex is a true waterproofing membrane designed to withstand hydrostatic pressure: the force of water-saturated soil pressing continuously against the foundation wall. This distinction matters enormously in practice. Dampproofing fails under pressure. Duraflex is engineered to hold against it.

This page explains what Duraflex is, how it works, why we chose it over competing products, and how it performs in the field conditions that Ontario foundations actually face. If you're comparing waterproofing proposals from different contractors, understanding the material differences will help you evaluate what you're actually being offered.

The Material

What Duraflex Is — and What Sets It Apart

Duraflex is not a commodity waterproofing product. Understanding its specific properties explains why it outperforms alternatives in real-world foundation conditions — and why those differences matter over the 20- to 30-year life of the system.

01

Rubberized Polymer Composition

Duraflex is a thick, rubberized membrane — not a paint, not a tar coat, not a thin spray. Its polymer composition gives it properties that rigid coatings fundamentally cannot replicate: flexibility, elongation, and the ability to move with the structure without cracking.

02

True Waterproofing vs Dampproofing

Dampproofing resists moisture vapour. Waterproofing resists liquid water under hydrostatic pressure. Duraflex is the latter. It is designed to hold against the force of water in saturated soil pressing continuously against the foundation wall — the actual condition foundations face after heavy rain and snowmelt.

03

Bonds to Damp Concrete

Most waterproofing products require a fully dry surface to bond correctly. Duraflex is specifically formulated to adhere to concrete that is still damp — a critical advantage when working with below-grade foundations that cannot always be fully dried before application.

04

Flexibility Through Freeze-Thaw Cycling

Ontario foundations move. Soil freezes, expands, and shifts. Concrete experiences minor thermal movement year-round. Rigid coatings crack at these movement points. Duraflex remains flexible, elongating with the structure rather than cracking — maintaining the waterproof seal through decades of freeze-thaw cycling.

05

Fills Surface Voids and Irregularities

Foundation walls are not smooth. They have form tie holes, honeycombed areas, cold joints, and surface pitting. Duraflex fills these rather than bridging over them — creating a continuous membrane that addresses the full surface condition rather than a thin layer that leaves voids behind.

06

New Construction Standard

Duraflex is specified by residential and commercial builders operating to current construction standards. When a new foundation is waterproofed correctly, Duraflex or an equivalent rubberized polymer membrane is what goes on the wall. Choosing it for existing home waterproofing brings that home to the same standard.

07

Compatible With Drainage Board Systems

Duraflex works as a system. A dimple drainage board is installed over the membrane, creating an air gap that protects the membrane from direct soil contact and directs water downward to the weeping tile. The membrane and drainage board are designed to function together — the drainage board cannot damage or delaminate the membrane.

08

Long Service Life

When properly applied to a well-prepared surface, Duraflex membranes are designed to last 20–30 years or more. This is consistent with what we see in the field: properly installed Duraflex membranes from projects completed a decade ago are still performing as specified, without cracking, delamination, or bond failure.

Application Process

How Duraflex Is Applied — The Process We Follow

The performance of any waterproofing membrane is determined as much by how it is applied as by the product itself. A quality membrane applied incorrectly will fail. Here is the process we follow on every Duraflex installation.

1. Surface Preparation

Duraflex performance begins with surface preparation. We remove all loose concrete, deteriorated material, and any remaining traces of old dampproofing or tar coatings from the wall face. Cold joints, form tie holes, and cracks are filled and rodded before membrane application. An improperly prepared surface is the single most common reason waterproofing membranes fail prematurely — and it's entirely within the installer's control.

Loose Material Removal Crack Pre-Fill Old Coating Removal

2. Prime Coat Application (Where Required)

On particularly porous or dusty concrete surfaces, a primer coat is applied before the membrane to improve bond strength. Priming ensures the Duraflex membrane grips the wall face completely rather than partially bonding to loose surface material.

Bond Primer Porous Concrete

3. Duraflex Membrane Application

The membrane is applied to the full height of the foundation wall from footing to above grade, ensuring complete coverage with no gaps, thin spots, or missed areas. Application thickness is controlled to specification — too thin and performance is compromised; Duraflex requires adequate thickness to deliver its rated flexibility and waterproofing performance.

Full Wall Coverage Controlled Thickness Footing to Grade

4. Drainage Board Installation

Once the membrane has cured, a dimple drainage board is installed over the full membrane surface. The board's dimple profile creates a continuous air gap between the soil and the membrane, allowing water to travel downward to the weeping tile rather than building hydrostatic pressure against the membrane surface. The drainage board also protects the membrane from stone and soil abrasion during backfill.

Dimple Board Air Gap Membrane Protection

5. Inspection Before Backfill

We inspect the full membrane surface and drainage board installation before any backfill is placed. Once the excavation is backfilled, the membrane is no longer accessible. This is the only opportunity to identify and correct any application issues — and we take it seriously on every project.

Field Observations

What We've Learned About Waterproofing Products in the Field

Years of excavating, assessing, and repairing foundations across Eastern Ontario has produced observations that don't appear in product literature. These are the patterns we see consistently — the realities of how waterproofing products actually perform over time in Ontario soil.

From our experience

We've excavated dozens of homes that had original tar dampproofing. In virtually every case, the coating had separated from the wall in sections — sometimes completely. The foundation had been unprotected for years without the homeowner knowing.

From our experience

A thin spray waterproofing product applied by a previous contractor had skinned over surface voids without filling them. When we removed it, we found intact air pockets behind the membrane — voids where water had been travelling unimpeded.

From our experience

One homeowner showed us photos of their previous exterior waterproofing — a product we didn't recognise — that had visibly cracked along the wall within two winters. Duraflex doesn't do that because it's not a rigid coating.

From our experience

We returned to a Duraflex application we completed 7 years prior for a related repair. The membrane was in perfect condition — fully bonded, uncracked, no delamination. That's consistent with what the product is specified to do.

From our experience

Hydraulic cement is a useful emergency stop, but we regularly inherit repairs where a homeowner patched a crack with hydraulic cement, the crack re-opened around the patch, and the patch itself became a leak point.

From our experience

Interior sealant paints — products sold at hardware stores for wet basements — cannot withstand hydrostatic pressure. We see them bubbling off walls or failing at seams within a season of application.

From our experience

The drainage board over the membrane is not optional. On a project where drainage board had been omitted by a previous contractor, the membrane had been physically abraded by angular backfill stone across 40% of the wall surface.

From our experience

Surface preparation is where most contractor shortcuts happen. We've removed Duraflex applied over existing tar by another contractor — the top membrane was fine, but it had never bonded to the wall because of what was underneath.

From our experience

A homeowner received three quotes: one for tar dampproofing, one for a thin spray, and one from us for Duraflex. The price difference was real but so is the product difference. You're not comparing the same thing.

From our experience

Duraflex applied to a properly prepared surface in 2018 was excavated for an adjacent repair in 2024. The membrane peeled back cleanly, completely intact — exactly what a rubberized polymer membrane should do after 6 years in Ontario soil.

Drainage board is a dimple-profile plastic sheet installed over the Duraflex membrane on the exterior of the foundation wall. The dimples create a continuous air gap between the backfill soil and the membrane surface. This gap serves two critical purposes: it allows water to drain freely downward to the weeping tile at the base of the footing — rather than building up as hydrostatic pressure against the membrane — and it protects the membrane from physical abrasion by angular stone and soil during and after backfill. Duraflex and drainage board are designed to work together as a system. The membrane without drainage board faces the full weight of saturated soil and the risk of physical damage. We never omit the drainage board on exterior foundation applications. It's not optional.

Duraflex is primarily specified for exterior foundation membrane applications — applied to the outside face of the foundation wall during excavation. This is the most direct and comprehensive approach to foundation waterproofing: the membrane stops water before it ever contacts the wall face. For interior waterproofing projects, we use a drainage channel system that intercepts water entering the foundation and directs it to a sump pump — a different but equally effective approach for the right situation. The right method depends on the specific water intrusion pattern, access conditions, foundation type, and project budget. We assess every foundation individually and recommend the approach that genuinely suits the problem.
